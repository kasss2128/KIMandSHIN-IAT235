Prouvé's
Design Philosophy

Jean Prouvé was a French industrial designer and architect known for his innovative approach to design and construction.

Functionality: Prouvé believed that design should prioritize function over form, rather than the other way around.

Rationality: Prouvé believed that industrial production could bring about greater efficiency and affordability in design.

Simplicity:Prouvé believed that simplicity was essential for good design, and that unnecessary decoration detracted from the function of an object.

Collaboration: Prouvé believed that collaboration was essential for successful designs, as different perspectives could lead to better solutions.

Prouvé's design philosophy focused on function, rationality, simplicity, and collaboration.

"Never design anything that cannot be made"

— Jean Prouvé

Career Timeline

  • 1901 born on April 8 in Nancy, France.
  • 1916-1918 attended École de Nancy, a school of decorative arts in France.
  • 1923 opened his own metalworking shop in Nancy.
  • 1924 began experimenting with furniture design.
  • 1929 collaborated with architect Robert Mallet-Stevens on the Maison de l'Homme project.
  • 1930 started his own architectural firm, Ateliers Jean Prouvé.
  • 1939 drafted into the French army and serves during World War II.
  • 1947 designed the famous Standard chair, which becomes an iconic piece of modern furniture design.
  • 1954 appointed director of the Conservatory of Arts and Crafts in Nancy.
  • 1957 designed the prefabricated Maison Tropicale for use in the French colonies.
  • 1971 died on March 23 in Nancy, France.

Charlotte Perriand

  • Design Philosophy

    Charlotte Perriand felt design should be accessible, functional, and adaptable to current living, informed by her time's social and cultural background. Her work emphasised simplicity, efficiency, and new materials and technology. Perriand felt that excellent design should serve society's needs and that designers should develop functional and beautiful items to better people's lives. She liked designing furniture and architecture for modern living and working. Perriand used steel, aluminium, and plastic to make lightweight, flexible furniture that was easy to produce and move. She also stressed ergonomics in design, making body-friendly furniture. Perriand's design philosophy of simplicity, functionality, and invention influenced modern design.

Charlotte Perriand's life

Charlotte Perriand (1903-1999) was a French architect and designer known for her innovative contributions to modernist design. She studied furniture design and worked with Le Corbusier and Pierre Jeanneret, designing iconic furniture pieces like the LC4 Chaise Longue. Perriand was committed to creating functional and adaptable designs using new materials and technologies. Her work in low-cost housing and ski lodges has had a lasting impact on modern design.

“Everything changes so quickly, and what is state of the art one moment won't be the next. Adaptation has to be ongoing – we have to know and accept this. These are transient times.”

—Charlotte Perriand
